Brand Kit For David / v1

How to make stuff that sounds like us.

Nate's Natty is cold process bar soap. Real ingredients. Fair price. No theater.

It started in a high school chemistry rabbit hole. Two years of R&D. Thousands of batches. The bar got good.

Then business school told me to charge a premium and build an "active lifestyle brand." I did, for a while. Hated it. The soap was great but nobody could afford it, and the whole thing felt like a costume.

So I pulled it back. Same formula. Same quality. Honest price. That's the version you're making content for.

Skim this now. Come back to it when you're stuck. Anything in here is up for discussion.

01 / Voice

Smart friend at a cookout.

Nate's Natty talks like a smart friend explaining something at a cookout. Not warm and huggy. Not corporate. Confident because the soap is good, not because we're posturing. Short sentences. Dry humor when it lands. Zero hype. We don't oversell. We respect the viewer's intelligence and we move on.

Sounds like us
  • Real soap at a fair price.
  • Four ingredients. That's it.
  • Most natural soap got expensive. Not better.
  • Good soap shouldn't cost luxury money.
Doesn't sound like us
  • Elevate your shower routine.
  • Crafted with love in small batches.
  • Your new self-care obsession.
  • The luxury bar you deserve.
  • A wellness ritual for the modern man.

Hard nos. These will make a Nate's Natty post feel like somebody else's brand.

No "drop" language. We don't drop scents on Friday. We just put them out.

No "luxury," "elevate," "ritual," "indulge," "treat yourself," "crafted." That's the industry we left.

No manufactured hype. No "you're not ready for this." No countdowns to nothing.

No influencer voice. If a TikTok influencer would say it, we don't.

No exclamation points unless the joke earns it. Almost never.

No em dashes. Use a hyphen with no space if you need one ("it's not a cosmetic- it's soap").

03 / Core Values

Four words we actually mean.

These aren't poster slogans. They are the filter for every decision, every post, every pricing call. If a piece of content doesn't reinforce at least one of these, it probably shouldn't go up.

01 Value

Good soap shouldn't cost luxury money. When our costs dropped, we cut the price 17%. No sale framing. We just lowered it.

02 Quality

Two years of R&D. Fatty acid profiles dialed by hand. The bar lasts longer, lathers better, and doesn't dry your skin out. The chemistry backs the talk.

03 Simplicity

Four ingredients in the base. Five in scented bars. Nothing extra. Fewer ingredients means fewer things that can mess with your skin.

04 Health

No parabens, sulfates, dyes, or synthetic fragrance. Cold process keeps the natural glycerin in the bar instead of stripping it out and selling it back to you in lotion.

04 / Visual Direction

Warm, grounded, not glossy.

Aesop's editorial confidence with warmer tones and more personality. The Ordinary's transparency. A craft coffee roaster's warmth without the "handmade" twee. Cinematic when we go big. Plain when we don't.

Photo & video

Tactile, natural light, hands and product.
Shoot this
Natural daylight. Real bathrooms. Hands holding the bar. Visible texture and swirls. Wooden mold, oils on the scale, the cutter mid-pull. Soap on a wet washcloth. The unedited version.
Skip this
White seamless backdrops. Flat lays with eucalyptus sprigs we don't grow. Heavy filter looks. Bathrooms that look like a magazine shoot. Anything that screams "DTC product photo."
05 / Content Pillars

Five starting ideas.

These aren't quotas to fill. They're jumping-off points to riff on. Each one branches a hundred ways. If something fits the brand and doesn't show up here, post it anyway.

01
Process / how it's made

Pouring batter, cutting fresh bars, weighing oils, the wooden mold. Satisfying mechanics. The "show the work" pillar.

02
Ingredient stories

Why olive oil. What glycerin actually does. Why most big-box soap strips it out. Short, plain, no chemistry-class energy.

03
Founder POV

Nate on camera. A take, a story, a customer email he's responding to. The brand has a face- use it. This is the trust pillar.

04
Customer use moments

Real people, real bathrooms, real reactions. The bar in someone's shower. A repeat customer's review. UGC over staged.

05
Scent of the week

One bar. One mood. One short caption. Lavender is the one everyone steals from you. Orange is morning person energy in bar form. Etc.

Mix is roughly: 40% process and ingredients, 30% founder POV, 20% customer/scent moments, 10% experimental trend stuff. Adjust by feel.

06 / Trends

Use them. If they earn it.

Trends are your strength. Use them. The filter is simple: the trend has to serve the substance. The trend is a vehicle for something true about the soap or the brand. If we're just doing the trend, the trend wins and the brand loses.

Trends that work

Works
"POV: you're a soap bar that doesn't strip your skin."
Close-up lather, hand-on-bar texture, slow pull. The trend earns the click. The benefit ("doesn't strip your skin") is the substance. Format and message agree.
Works
"Tell me you engineered your soap without telling me."
Cut between fatty acid notebook, scale, raw bars on the cutter, the wooden mold. The trend is a frame for showing the work. We're not bragging, we're demonstrating.
Works
"What I bought vs. what they sell." Side-by-side bar comparison.
The bar lasting longer than a syndet. Visible difference. No trash talk. Just the comparison, plainly. Anchor in the price-cut story if it lands.

Trends that feel forced

Forced
Dance trend with a bar of soap awkwardly in frame.
The trend is the whole bit. The soap is decoration. We aren't selling the dance.
Forced
"The new scent DROPS Friday" countdown energy.
Wrong voice. We don't drop. We don't manufacture hype. We just put it out and tell people.
Forced
GRWM where the soap shows up for two seconds at the end.
Hollow. The viewer didn't learn anything, didn't see anything, and the soap is a tag-on. If you do GRWM, the soap has to be the moment, not the disclosure.
07 / Workflow

First few weeks, then handover.

The first stretch is calibration, not micromanagement. Once your voice and mine line up, you post directly. We move fast. Cadence is flexible, quality over volume.

01
Drafts to me first.

For the first few weeks, send drafts before they go up. Caption, hook, rough cut. I'll come back fast. This is voice calibration, not approval theater.

02
We talk about why.

If I change something, I'll tell you why. So you can carry the reasoning forward. The goal is for you to internalize the voice, not memorize my edits.

03
Handover.

Once we're aligned, you post directly. I stay in the loop on bigger swings (founder appearances, anything that touches price or product claims). You own the day-to-day.

04
Cadence is flexible.

No fixed posting quota. If we have something to say, we post. If we don't, we don't fake it. Better to have one strong post a week than five empty ones.
